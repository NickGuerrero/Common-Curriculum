Set Up Your Search Experiment — 10 + 10

Learning goals

  • Turn your job search into a fair experiment you can actually learn from, not a numbers game.
  • Leave with a runnable plan: a geography bet, 10 + 10 applications, and the signal you’ll watch.

This week you test one variable: geography — where you aim. You’ll set up a fair test between the region you’d default to and a region you have ties to, send 10 applications into each, and watch what comes back. A “no” or silence is data, not failure.

What to do

  1. Pick your two regions.
    A · Control

    The region you’d default to — the one everyone applies to.

    B · Experimental

    A region you have ties to: you’re from or near there, or it has an economic sector unique to that region that fits you.

  2. Write your bet — by hand first, then type it. One clear line:
    Your bet

    Aiming at [region B] beats only chasing [region A], because [your real reason to win B: you’re local, or your background fits the region’s industry].

  3. Make it a fair test. Use one resume for both regions, built to make your reason to win B visible (your location, or how your background fits the region’s sector). Change only the region — otherwise a “no” in B won’t tell you anything.
  4. Find your 10 + 10. Line up 10 real postings in region A and 10 in region B, each a genuine fit. Aim for 3–5 quality (not spray-and-pray) per day across the week. A few quick ones on top are fine — just keep them separate. Use your Search Strategy Dojo to help (below).
  5. Name your skill-to-learn. As you read the postings, watch for the one technology or process that keeps showing up. Pick it, and note how you’ll start learning it on your own — that’s how you close the gap your search reveals.
  6. Log everything and start sending. Record each application in your Search Log (a new tab on your application tracker), then start sending. Responses come in over the next two weeks.

Use your Search Strategy Dojo

In your AI Dojo

Add the Search Strategy module to your Dojo (Dojo library), then let it coach you: your two regions, your bet, the fair-test setup, your 10 + 10, and the signal you’ll watch. At the end, paste its summary block straight into your Search Log — no re-typing.

Worked example — one student’s geography bet

Andre — backend / data niche.

Bet: “Salinas Valley agtech — a sector that’s big here and almost nowhere else, where my data-pipeline work fits — beats only chasing the crowded Bay Area.”

Fair test: one resume that leads with his pipeline, framed for the industry, sent to 10 Bay Area general roles (A) and 10 Salinas agtech roles (B).

What came back: Bay Area, silence (lost in the pile); agtech, 2 replies — one asked about his pipeline directly.

Lesson + adjustment: the Bay Area silence wasn’t about him, it was the crowded lane; the region-unique sector read his background as directly relevant. He shifted weight toward his tie region and sharpened the resume line that named the local-industry fit.

Before you submit, check four things
  • Is B specific? A real region with a real reason you’d win there — not “somewhere cheaper.”
  • Could a peer tell A from B? If the two groups blur together, it isn’t a test yet.
  • One resume, edge visible? Same resume to both, with your reason to win B on the page.
  • Is your signal real? Name what you’ll actually watch (reply rate, a recruiter reply, an interview) — and remember silence is data.

Capture & submit

Make a copy of your Search Log (a new Search Strategy tab on your application tracker) and fill in: region (A or B), your bet, your reason to win B, what you sent and when, the signal you’re watching, and your skill-to-learn.

Submit: the link to your Search Log (set sharing so anyone with the link can view). Then start sending — 3–5 quality applications a day.
