Resume Story Swap

Learning goal

See where your resume drifts from — or is silent on — your niche, and leave with concrete fixes for your Resume v2.

Every resume tells a story, whether you meant it to or not. Today you say yours out loud, and a partner checks it against the niche you have been developing.

Two ways the story breaks:

Drift  Your resume points somewhere other than your niche — the loudest items aim at a different role.

Gap  Your resume is silent on your niche — nothing on the page proves you belong in it yet.

How it works (~20 min)

  1. Set up. Pair up. Open your current resume and your Niche Doc one-liner.
  2. Speaker (~7 min). Say your niche in one sentence, then tell the story your resume tells in about 90 seconds — the through-line, not a top-to-bottom read. Your partner listens with the lens below.
  3. Name the breaks. Together, find 2–3 points of drift or gap. For each, decide: tighten the resume, or refine the niche.
  4. Swap and repeat.
  5. Capture (~3 min). Each person fills in the reflection below and drops it into their Niche Doc — Week 2 update.
The listener’s lens
  • Does the resume’s through-line point at the stated niche?
  • Which items support the niche? Which are off-target or missing?
  • Where would a recruiter for that niche get confused or unconvinced?

See it in action

Below is a standard, well-formatted tech resume — but notice it has no niche. It says “I can do a bit of everything,” which is exactly the problem.

A solid resume with no niche — what story does it tell?
Your Name
B.S. Computer Science · Arizona State University (expected 2026)
Highlights
  • Participated in 4 hackathons
  • VP of Finance, Software Engineering club
  • Self-taught coder since high school
Technical Skills
Python, Swift, JavaScript, Node.js, APIs
Projects
  • iOS habit-tracker app (Swift) — built the UI with a teammate
  • Group web app (Node.js, React) — worked on the team
Experience
  • Math tutor — helped students with calculus

Story it tells: “I’m a CS student who has tried a lot of things.” A recruiter cannot tell what role this person is aiming for.

Now give it a niche — and the drift & gaps appear

Niche one-liner: “Backend engineer for data-heavy products — APIs, databases, and performance.”

Drift The loudest items — the iOS app and “VP of Finance” — point at mobile and leadership, not backend. They sit at the top and pull the story away from the niche.

Gap Nothing on the page mentions databases, scale, latency, or data volume. The niche claims backend and data, but there is no evidence for it yet.

Fixing one bullet — before & after
Before · drifts & vague
Group web app (Node.js, React) — worked on the team.
After · points at the niche
Designed the Node.js/Express API and Postgres schema for a 3-person app; added Redis caching that cut median response time from 800ms to 120ms.

Same project — but the rewrite leads with backend ownership, names the data layer, and proves performance impact. That is what closes the gap.
