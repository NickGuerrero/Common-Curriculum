Career Intelligence · Week 2 · Develop & Articulate Your Niche

Curate & Analyze 10 Jobs Aligned to Your Niche

Learning goals

  • Find real jobs that fit your niche so your search targets roles you can actually land.
  • Practice explaining out loud why a role fits you — the kind of case you’ll make in an interview.

What to do

  1. Start from the job-search prompt below — fill in your niche from your Niche Doc (what you bring, your value, the role types you’re testing) and run a first search.
  2. Read the postings it brings back and ask follow-up questions — redirect where they’re off, widen where you’re too narrow, tighten where you’re too broad — to work toward the best fits.
  3. As patterns emerge, name your 2–3 role types. If a type is still thin, search more — don’t force three out of one batch of ten.
  4. Land on 10 real postings that represent your 2–3 role types, log them in the tracker, and pull the requirements they repeat.
Job-search prompt — fill in your niche

Here is my niche so far, from my Niche Doc:
• What I bring: [your intersection — the unusual combination, e.g. “backend engineering + a stats background”]
• The value I want to offer: [your draft value statement, 1–2 lines]
• Role types I’m testing: [2–3 you’re considering, including one stretch]

Help me turn this into a real job search — work with me, don’t just hand me a list:
1. Pull a handful of real, currently-open postings that fit, and for each show the company, title, link, and the exact phrases that match my niche.
2. Ask me follow-up questions — where does this fit, where doesn’t it, am I too broad or too narrow? — and use my answers to redirect and widen or tighten the search.
3. As patterns emerge, help me name 2–3 role types. If a type is thin, search wider — don’t force three out of the first ten.
4. Once the 2–3 role types are solid, find 10 real postings that represent them, spread across the types.
Only use postings you can link to — don’t invent any.

Three students, three filled-in niches

A — machine learning + public-good data

What I bring: ML plus a pull toward public-good data — I built a model that predicted city bus delays from open transit data, and spent a summer tagging sensor recordings at a research lab.

The value I offer: I turn messy real-world data into models people can act on, and I care whether the data is trustworthy — not just the accuracy score.

Role types I’m testing: applied ML / data engineer at civic- or climate-tech companies; a research-data role at a lab or museum; stretch — ML engineer at a mission-driven startup.

B — frontend + HCI research

What I bring: Frontend engineering plus human-computer-interaction research — I built a tool that maps community-college courses to UC transfer requirements and ran the user interviews behind it (the work became a published paper).

The value I offer: I build interfaces grounded in real user research, so what I ship solves the problem people actually have, not the one we assumed.

Role types I’m testing: frontend engineer at an edtech company; UX engineer or UX researcher; stretch — product engineer at an early-stage startup.

C — embedded + hardware

What I bring: Embedded systems plus two years of hands-on hardware — I built a sensor-packed rover and worked on a prosthetics project, writing the firmware and soldering it myself.

The value I offer: I’m at home where software meets the physical world, and I’ve shipped real devices, not just demos — a flaky sensor at 2am is my idea of a good problem.

Role types I’m testing: embedded / firmware engineer at a medical-device or robotics company; IoT software engineer; stretch — R&D engineer on an assistive-tech team.

In the live session — work in pairs

One of you drives: share your screen, run the prompt, pull up postings. The other helps by pushing on each one — then swap. Good helper questions:

  • “Where’s the niche language in this posting — can you point to the exact words?”
  • “Is this a real match, or are we forcing it? What’s the evidence you’d fit?”
  • “Which of your 2–3 role types does this belong to, and why that one?”
Three checks
  • Niche explicit? If you can’t point to the words that match your niche, it’s a generic job — swap it.
  • Can’t find 10? Your niche may be too narrow — adjust rather than force-fit.
  • Real role types? If a group is just a list of companies, say what the work actually is — or merge it into another type.

Capture & submit

Make a copy of the job tracker, then log your 10 niche-aligned jobs — company, position, status, link, the requirements they repeat, and which of your 2–3 role types each belongs to.

Submit: the link to your copy of the tracker (set sharing so anyone with the link can view).
