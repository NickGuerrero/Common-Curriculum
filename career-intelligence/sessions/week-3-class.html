Week 3 · Live session

Resume Review Workshop

A full resume workshop led by Jeff — turning the resume you built in Week 2 into one that proves you fit a specific role, ready for Own Your Search in Week 4.

What to bring

Bring these so you have something real to work on:

  • Your current resume — the Resume v2 you built in Week 2.
  • One real job posting you’re targeting — ideally one you’ve already tailored your resume toward.
  • Your Niche Doc — your 2–3 role types and the evidence behind them.

Three questions to consider

Be ready for a full resume workshop led by Jeff. Here are three questions to consider as you review your resume:

  1. If a recruiter reads only the top third of your resume for ten seconds, what specifically tells them you fit this role — and what’s missing?
  2. What is the strongest piece of evidence on your resume for the role you’re targeting, and which claim is weakest or hardest to back up?
  3. Which one bullet will you rewrite first, and what specific result or number will you add to make it stronger?

1 · Why Resumes Matter & Today’s Plan ~4.5 min

One shot at a first impression, and the “make a different adjustment” mindset.

Takeaways

  • You get one shot at a first impression — the resume is what earns the interview.
  • If you’re not getting hits, it’s about making adjustments — like a dartboard, “miss differently” until you hit the bullseye.
  • Jeff’s take comes from recruiting at Microsoft and TikTok, plus a meeting with Ashby an hour before.

2 · Psychology of the Recruiter: The Top Third ~6.5 min

The 3–5 second scan, and why the top third of the page decides yes or no.

Takeaways

  • Recruiters scan in roughly 3–5 seconds and start trending to a yes or no almost immediately.
  • There’s a resume “pattern” recruiters say yes to — fit the pattern first, then break it.
  • The top third of the page is your best shot at their full, neutral attention — use it deliberately.

3 · What to Put in the Top Third ~3 min

Relevant experience, achievements with context, and passion projects.

Takeaways

  • A prior “software engineer intern” title is the holy grail, but achievements with context work too (top 3 of how many?).
  • Frame yourself as an achiever — a “Selected Achievements” line calls out your best so the recruiter doesn’t have to fish.
  • Passion projects can become near-automatic first-round interviews (Jeff saw this at Microsoft and TikTok).

4 · The Human Element (Your Tiebreaker) ~3.5 min

Why a human touch breaks ties in an AI-flooded pile — the F1-marshal story.

Takeaways

  • Remote work and AI mean more applications that all read the same — a human touch is the tiebreaker.
  • Add one genuine, often non-CS detail about what you love (the F1-marshal example).
  • It works: that candidate’s recruiter screen opened with the F1 detail, and he landed the Amazon job.

5 · Live: North-Star the Job Description ~7 min

Pulling the top three “wants” from a real posting before touching the resume.

Takeaways

  • Before touching your resume, read the posting and pull the top three “wants” the hiring manager is seeking.
  • A role exists to solve a problem — figure out what they want, then give your example of it.
  • For the camp-coordinator role, the three were leadership, communication, and management/organization.

6 · Live: Build a Highlights Section ~9.5 min

Turning those wants into a “quality : evidence” block at the top of the resume.

Takeaways

  • Swap “Education” at the top for a “Highlights” block: a few bullets of quality : evidence (Leadership: your real example…).
  • Don’t make the recruiter fish — “start your dinner with dessert” by putting your best up top.
  • A structure to try: a one-sentence “you + this company” opener, then three highlights, punctuated by a human element.

7 · Q&A: How Many, What Order & Showing You Care ~10 min

Bullet count, section ordering, and signaling genuine interest.

Takeaways

  • Three to four highlights look cleanest (the brain handles about three); experiment — “the one rule is there are no rules.”
  • Lead with whatever’s most relevant — real job or internship experience over class projects; label it “technical experience,” not “work experience.”
  • Ashby’s data says recruiters want to see you genuinely care about the work — so say it explicitly.

8 · Emblems, the “Closer” & Your Exercise ~10 min

Extreme interests, recruiter language like “closer,” then the do-it-yourself instructions.

Takeaways

  • An “emblem” is an extreme interest (a poker player, an outrigger-canoe team) that shows you go deep — not a generic hobby.
  • Use recruiter language like “closer” — give an example of driving something across the finish line.
  • Your turn: pull the top three wants from a real posting, write a specific example for each, and be detailed (AI screens on keywords).

9 · Debrief: A Student Example ~7.5 min

A peer applies it end-to-end with live feedback.

Takeaways

  • Shawn shares his highlights — programming, team player, and a “closer” example (a bus-delay predictor he kept building).
  • Jeff’s feedback: add concrete context (best in 3–4 years), and mention “team” at the end of a bullet, not the start.
  • Continuing a class project on your own can become a passion project — and demonstrated interest always helps.

10 · Wrap-Up & Closing Tips ~9.5 min

Accomplishment vs. process statements, the Dojo, and free-response & portfolio tips.

Takeaways

  • Resumes should be mostly accomplishment statements, not process statements (Laszlo Bock’s quality-then-evidence formula).
  • Aim for the bar: in the first third, a reader should already want to talk to you — the Dojo can help you get there.
  • Two Ashby tips: answer free-response questions yourself (not ChatGPT), and a personal portfolio frees you from the one-page limit.
