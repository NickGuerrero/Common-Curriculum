Inside Hiring with Ashby

Kevin Connolly leads data and recruiting analytics at Ashby — an applicant tracking system used by 4,500+ companies, from OpenAI and Shopify to two-person startups. In this guest session he answers the questions students most want answered about hiring and AI, with real data from across thousands of recruiting teams.

His talk is cut into eight short clips, each with one learning goal. Watch in order for the full arc, or jump to what you need — clips 1–4 build the mental model, clips 5–8 are the practical playbook. Captions are on by default. Total run time ≈ 33 minutes.

1 · The Insider's View 3.5 min

Learning goal

Adopt the talent team's perspective — the mental model behind every later lesson.

Key takeaways

  • Kevin's path: physics PhD → 10+ years in startups → 5.5 years at Ashby, leading its data team and recruiting analytics aggregated across thousands of companies.
  • The key reframe: a candidate thinks "I'm a good fit." The talent team thinks "we have 1,500 applicants, ~100 are great, and we can only talk to 20."
  • Ashby's public "Talent Trends" reports are the source of the data charts you'll see in later clips.
Discuss. How does seeing hiring from the recruiter's side change how you'd write an application? Why might being a genuinely good fit still not be enough?

2 · Why Hiring Changed 4.5 min

Learning goal

Understand the post-COVID shifts and why AI screening is framed as candidate-friendly.

Key takeaways

  • Remote work opened jobs to national and global applicant pools, so applications per job roughly tripled.
  • Recruiters use AI at the "top of funnel" to avoid missing good candidates; AI interviewing can give more people an at-bat (e.g., 40 instead of 20).
  • Hiring fraud is now a real concern in remote roles. A thin or mismatched profile (no LinkedIn, VPN, locations that don't line up) won't auto-reject you, but can flag you for review — transparency helps.
Discuss. Is AI screening good or bad for applicants? Argue both sides. What can you do to not look like a suspicious profile?

3 · The Human Behind the AI 4.3 min

Learning goal

Understand human-in-the-loop assessment and how expectations are shifting.

Key takeaways

  • AI scores candidates against job criteria for speed and comprehensiveness, but a human always makes the final decision — Ashby never lets AI auto-reject. The "I changed one keyword and got rejected, so it's an evil system" story is usually stressed recruiters, not the AI.
  • Because everyone now uses AI on take-home tasks, answers converge — so recruiters increasingly test raw critical thinking and fundamentals (you'll be piloting these tools on the job).
  • Application-volume and fraud spikes are concentrated in high-value tech; the picture is less certain for non-tech roles.
Discuss. If everyone's take-home answers look the same with AI, how should employers assess real skill? What's the line between using AI as a tool and letting it do your thinking?

4 · Why Great Candidates Don't Get a Screen 2.4 min

Learning goal

Read the real data and depersonalize rejection.

Key takeaways

  • Applications per hire rose from ~100 (2021) to ~300; Kevin's own recent data hire drew ~1,500 applicants.
  • On average, only ~18 applicants per role get a scheduled interview.
  • The bottleneck is the top of the funnel: teams optimize for "picking the top 20," not screening everyone — so a strong candidate can be cut without a call. It's a numbers game, not always a verdict on you.
Discuss. If only ~18 of 300 get interviewed, what's the smartest way to spend your application effort? How should this change where you apply?

5 · Using AI the Right Way 4.6 min

Learning goal

Navigate AI-in-interview norms and avoid AI-written applications backfiring.

Key takeaways

  • Two camps: some teams say "use AI in our process — we want to see how you use it," others say "no AI, we want the raw you." Norms aren't settled, so clarify when you can.
  • AI-generated free-form answers are easy to spot at volume; Kevin says he can quickly reject hundreds for obvious LLM phrasing. A half-hearted application reads as "doesn't care."
  • The winning move: research companies you genuinely care about and show it. Apply selectively where you'll stand out rather than blasting 100 generic applications.
Discuss. When is using AI in an application helpful vs. harmful? How do you show real interest in a company without sounding generic?

6 · How Long Hiring Takes 2.5 min

Learning goal

Set realistic expectations about timelines and process.

Key takeaways

  • Median time-to-hire for engineering roles ≈ 40 days (average closer to 45–50); it's fairly stable, driven mostly by scheduling gaps between steps.
  • A typical structured tech process: recruiter screen → screen → assessment/take-home → on-site panel → offer (steps sometimes reordered), often with a background check.
  • Candidates who get hired do ~3.5–4 hours of interviews on average, with outliers up to ~7.
Discuss. Knowing it takes roughly six weeks, how should you manage multiple applications at once? How do you stay engaged during long gaps without being pushy?

7 · Resumes, Portfolios & New Roles 7 min

Learning goal

Make the "hard-decision" factors work in your favor.

Key takeaways

  • At the top of the funnel recruiters have the least information, so marginal details decide: be specific, not vague; typos and fluff hurt when 25–30 strong candidates compete.
  • A personal portfolio site is now nearly free to build with AI — a high-value differentiator if it works (broken links hurt more than no site). Use it to show personality, interests, and projects.
  • Note: Ashby's default review doesn't auto-click portfolio links (a human does), so make them easy to find. Emerging role to watch: the "forward-deployed engineer" at AI firms helping companies implement AI.
Discuss. What three specific things would you put on a portfolio site for your target role? Rewrite one vague resume bullet to be concrete and verifiable.

8 · Do's & Don'ts: Networking & Rejection 4.4 min

Learning goal

A practical outreach and resilience playbook.

Key takeaways

  • Do research companies deeply and read many postings for a role to map its core skills and your own gaps. Don't cold-ask busy hiring managers for "a quick chat" during active hiring — send a "zero-ask" note instead ("I've read the role, I'm excited, hope we get to talk — I understand if not").
  • Don't self-select out: you can express interest even when no role is posted (follow on LinkedIn/X, join talent communities) — it's far easier for a manager to engage when they aren't buried in applications.
  • Know the company cold on calls (a wrong assumption can fail you instantly). Take rejection gracefully — Kevin has rejected people and then hired them months later; an angry reply shuts the door for good.
Discuss. Draft a three-sentence "zero-ask" outreach message to a company you admire. Why might a rejection sometimes be "dodging a bullet"?
One throughline. Almost every clip comes back to the same idea: hiring is a high-volume, time-pressured process, so the candidates who get noticed are the ones who make a recruiter's hard decision easy — be specific, be genuine, and target the places you actually care about.
