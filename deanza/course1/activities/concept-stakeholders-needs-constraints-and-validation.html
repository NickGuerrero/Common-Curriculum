Concept: Stakeholders, Needs, Constraints, And Validation

Learning goal

Understand Concept: Stakeholders, Needs, Constraints, And Validation and connect it to the work in this module.

Stakeholders Are Specific

A stakeholder is not just a category like users or leadership. In your problem context, stakeholders are people or roles with a specific relationship to the problem. They may be affected by the problem, able to influence it, responsible for a constraint, or positioned to challenge your frame.

Impact, Influence, And Authority Differ

A person can be highly affected with little authority. Another person can approve a change but rarely experience the problem directly. Good stakeholder work keeps these differences visible because each perspective tells you something different.

Validation Is Listening

Validation means testing the problem frame, not pitching a solution. Strong validation questions invite the stakeholder to describe what they experience, need, fear, value, or see differently. If your questions are designed to make them agree with you, you are not validating.

Conflicts Are Evidence

Stakeholder perspectives may conflict. Do not smooth over the tension just to make the report cleaner. Tension can reveal scope, risk, authority, incentives, or evidence that your first frame missed.

Practice This In Your Work

  • List at least one affected stakeholder, one influential stakeholder, and one authority or constraint stakeholder.
  • Mark each need as known, inferred, or validated.
  • Write questions that invite stories and examples.
  • Revise the problem based on evidence, not preference.

Check Your Understanding

Before you move to the next activity, write one sentence in your notes that names the decision you need to make next. If the sentence could apply to anyone, add more of your own context.
